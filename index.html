How to read it

A quick legend for viewers.

λ

Horizontal axis

Wavelength is plotted in micrometers, written µm. The table also keeps nanometers for spreadsheet readability.

PW

Vertical axis

Peak power is shown in petawatts on a logarithmic scale, so each major step is a multiplication.

About this project

Why the site exists.

This catalogue collects high-peak-power laser systems and makes their record values easier to compare across wavelength, technology, country, repetition rate, and source quality. The goal is a small, reliable, traceable dataset rather than a large table of unchecked numbers.

Laser/facility, Institution, Country, Wavelength, Pulse energy, Pulse duration [fs], Peak power, Rep. rate, Year demonstrated, Laser technology, Reference DOI/link, Notes, Description

Data Validation

Data quality rules

What the verification flags mean.

1

Every major number should trace to a source

Use a DOI, paper, report, or facility page for peak power, wavelength, energy, duration, and repetition rate.

2

Reported vs computed peak power

If the paper does not report peak power directly, the loader can compute it as energy divided by duration. The row will be flagged.

3

Missing values stay visible

Rows without wavelength or peak power remain in the table and health panel, but they cannot appear on the plot yet.

Known limitations

Good to mention in your presentation.

!

Mixed data types

The spreadsheet may include demonstrated, design, planned, and commissioning values. The status filter separates them.

≈

Ranges and estimates

Values like “0.4–1.7 kJ” or “estimated” are simplified for plotting. Keep the original wording in Notes.

↗

The plot is not a final record book

It is a research aid. The table and sources matter as much as the visual points.
